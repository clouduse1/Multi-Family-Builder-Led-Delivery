Construction Loans and Draw Schedules Explained

By the Editorial Team | Published: July 2026 | Last Updated: July 2026

How a Building Gets Paid For While It Rises

A finished apartment building can be worth tens of millions, but on the day construction starts it is worth only the land and a set of drawings. Financing a project through that gap - funding months of construction before a single unit earns rent - requires a distinctive instrument: the construction loan. Unlike a mortgage on a finished building, a construction loan releases money gradually as the building takes shape, and the mechanics of that release govern the project's cash flow, schedule, and stress. Anyone involved in delivering multi-family work needs to understand how construction financing actually moves, because the money's rhythm sets the project's rhythm.

Why Construction Loans Work Differently

A lender will not hand a developer the full construction budget on day one, because on day one there is nothing built to secure it. Instead the loan is committed up front but disbursed in installments - called draws - released only as verified work is completed. The building itself is the collateral, and the lender advances money against value that physically exists at each stage. This protects the lender, who never has more money out than there is building to back it, and it disciplines the project, since each dollar must be justified by progress before it arrives.

The Draw Schedule

The draw schedule is the agreed plan for how and when the loan releases funds, tied to construction milestones or to a regular cycle of verified progress. A typical structure releases money as phases complete - foundation, framing, dry-in, mechanical rough-in, finishes - with each draw requiring documentation and often an inspection before the lender funds it.

Draw stage (typical)Released when
Sitework & foundationExcavation and foundations verified complete

Dry-in & rough-insWeather-tight; mechanical/electrical/plumbing roughed
FinishesInterior finishes progress toward completion
Final / retainage releaseCompletion, certificate of occupancy, punch list

Each draw request is supported by documentation - a schedule of values showing what portion of each line item is complete, lien waivers from subcontractors confirming they have been paid, and often a third-party inspection verifying the work exists. The lender funds the draw only after this package satisfies it, which is why draw administration is a real job on a multi-family project, not a formality.

Retainage and the Cash-Flow Squeeze

Two features make construction financing a constant balancing act. First, retainage: the lender (and the developer, from subcontractors) typically withholds a percentage of each payment - often five to ten percent - until the project is complete, ensuring everyone stays motivated to finish. That withheld money is earned but not yet paid, so the builder carries it. Second, the timing gap: work must be performed and often paid for before the draw reimbursing it arrives, so the builder and subcontractors front costs and wait. A project that runs draws smoothly keeps everyone liquid; one that lets documentation lag or work outpace the schedule creates a cash-flow squeeze that can stall the job even when the loan itself is sound.

Why Financing Fluency Is a Delivery Skill

The draw rhythm is why construction financing belongs in any discussion of project delivery rather than being walled off in a finance department. A builder who understands what each draw requires - the documentation, the inspections, the lien waivers - assembles clean draw packages that fund on time and keep the project's cash moving. One who treats the loan as someone else's problem discovers the schedule stalling while everyone waits on a rejected draw. Financing fluency, in other words, is part of building well, and coordinating it with construction is a core competency of builder-led delivery.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a construction loan?

A loan that funds a project's construction in stages rather than all at once, releasing money as verified work is completed. The building itself is the collateral, and the lender advances funds against the value that physically exists at each stage.

What is a draw schedule?

The agreed plan for how and when a construction loan releases funds, tied to milestones or verified progress. Money is released as phases complete - foundation, framing, dry-in, finishes - with each draw requiring documentation and often inspection before funding.

What documentation does a draw require?

Typically a schedule of values showing completion of each line item, lien waivers from subcontractors confirming payment, and often a third-party inspection verifying the work exists. The lender funds the draw only after this package satisfies it.

What is retainage?

A percentage of each payment - commonly five to ten percent - withheld until the project is complete, keeping everyone motivated to finish. It is money earned but not yet paid, which the builder and subcontractors carry until the final release.

Why do builders have to front costs?

Because work is performed, and often paid for, before the draw reimbursing it arrives. This timing gap means builders and subcontractors advance costs and wait for the loan to catch up, which is why smooth draw administration matters so much to cash flow.

What happens if a draw is delayed or rejected?

The project can stall for lack of cash even when the loan is fundamentally sound, because the money to continue is stuck. Incomplete documentation, work outpacing the verified schedule, or failed inspections are common causes - which is why clean draw packages are essential.

Why is construction financing part of project delivery?

Because the schedule runs on the rhythm of the draws. A builder who understands each draw's requirements keeps funds flowing and the project liquid; treating the loan as a separate concern invites stalls. Coordinating financing with construction is a core delivery skill.

What happens to the construction loan when the building is finished?

It is typically replaced by permanent financing - a longer-term mortgage on the completed, income-producing building, in a step often called the takeout. The construction loan is short-term and higher-cost by design, so refinancing it into permanent debt once the building earns rent lowers the carrying cost and stabilizes the project financially. Reaching that takeout sooner is a major reason developers push to complete and lease up efficiently.

What is a schedule of values?

A document that breaks the total contract price into line items - foundation, framing, mechanical, finishes, and so on - each assigned a dollar value. At each draw, the builder marks what percentage of each line item is complete, and that becomes the basis for the amount requested. It gives the lender and the developer a clear, itemized view of progress against money, and keeping it accurate is central to clean draw administration.
